readtime1 <- cbind(letterrec3,beginning3,ending3,words3,reading3) ~ 1

The two-class model is preferred over the one-class model on the basis of the BIC. The results show that Class 1 is made up of children who have all of the precursor skills to full reading, but are not quite mastering reading words separately and in context. These children make up approximately 27% of the sample. Class 2 is made of up children in the fall of 1st grade who have mastered letter recognition and beginning sounds as well as a majority who have mastered ending sounds. These children make up approximately 73% of the sample. Recall that the sum of the probabilities across individuals within each latent class gives the predicted class membership The same data can be used to uniquely assign an individual to a latent class based on the highest probability (then coded 1, the other classes coded zero for that individual). The proportion of 1s in a latent class provides the estimated class population shares. The predicted class memberships and estimated class population shares should be close, indicating a good solution.

Compare entropy values.

Entropy is a number taking a minumum value of 0 (representing complete concentration of probability on one cell) and a maximum value equal to the logarithm of the total number of cells in the fitted cross-classfication table (representing complete dispersion, or equal probability for outcomes across every cell). Thus, lower values indicate better separation.

poLCA.entropy(m1)
## [1] 2.047053
poLCA.entropy(m2)
## [1] 1.800625
poLCA.entropy(m3)
## [1] 1.72894

We find that Model 3 shows the clearest class separation as measured by the entropy coefficient, but it is not much lower than the two-class model.

First, note that the class labels have switched. Class 1 is the high level reading skill class, Class 2 is the precursor reading skill class, and Class 3 is the low reading skill class. Also, the class proportions have changed a bit, indicating the importance of SES as a predictor of class membership. The bottom of the output is a multinomial regression. The predictor is SES, and the outcome are the log odds of membership in group 2 v. group 1 and also group 3 v. group 1. We find that higher levels of SES are associated with lower log odds of being in the precursor reading skill class compared to the high skill class. Also, higher levels of SES are associated with even lower log odds of being in the low reading skill class compared to the high reading skill class. These can be converted to odds ratios if you wish. So, \(e^{2.018}\) = 7.52 So, a one unit increaase in SES is associated with a 7.5-fold increase in the odds being in the high-skill reading group compared to the low-skill reading group. Similarly, \(e^{1.400}\) = 4.06, meaning that a one unit increase in SES is associated with a 4-fold increase in the odds of being in the precursor reading group compared to the low-skill reading group.
